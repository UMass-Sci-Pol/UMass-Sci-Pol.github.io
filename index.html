What we do

We are one of the first undergraduate-centered science policy and advocacy initiatives in the United States. Our goals are to:

  • Expose students across all disciplines to the world of science policy and advocacy.

  • Empower students to engage in real-world issues at the intersection of science, society, and policy.

  • Enable students to experience the science policy process in practice.

  • Provide resources, mentoring, and support for students interested in science policy and/or advocacy efforts and integrating these into their careers.

Who we are

We are a student-run organization and we are looking for more members to form an organization that will be stable in the long term.

Kenneth Lin '20, Co-Director

I am majoring in physics and astronomy, and have been involved in astrophysics research since my first year at UMass where I focus on galaxy evolution and advanced optical systems that can be used for both astrophysics and medical imaging. My interests in policy stem from my early involvement in high school with activities such as student government and representing students at the MA state legislature. Combining my interests in science and governance, I realized the significant lack of science representation in policymaking limits the pace and directions of research as a consequence of funding and national priorities. Working in research groups in the U.S. and abroad, I found that the deficit in scientific expertise in government is quite universal. I hope to help change that by motivating us to be more active advocates for ethical and effective science.

Joseph McGaunn '20, Co-Director

I am majoring in biochemistry/molecular biology and psychology, and my research focus is on epigenetics, the “software” of cells that allow organisms to integrate environmental and genetic information, as well as pass down non-genetic information to offspring in some cases. I first saw the importance of science in policy making as a member of my high school’s Model UN team, and since then have been interested in facilitating communication between science and policymakers on issues such as science funding and education, gene editing, and the potential legal, political, and ethical implications of epigenetic research. I hope to facilitate evidence-based policymaking and ethical research throughout my career as a physician-scientist.
